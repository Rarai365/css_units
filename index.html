What is absoulute Unit and Relative Unit in CSS?

In CSS, absolute units and relative units are used to define sizes, like the width, height, margin, padding, and font sizes, and they behave differently based on the surrounding context.

Absoulute Units

Absolute units are fixed measurements that do not change based on other elements or the viewport. They are static and offer precise control over size. Common absolute units include:

  1. px:Pixels -a fixed, screen based mesurement.
  2. cm: Centimeters- a real -world physical measurement.
  3. mm:Millimeters-also a real -world measurement.
  4. in:inces
  5. pt:Points-traditionlly 1/72 of an inch, used in print design.
  6. pc:Picas-1 pica is equal to 12 points anotehr print design unit.

Realtive Units

Relative units scale in response to their context, meaning they adapt to the parent element or viewport. This makes them useful for responsive design. Common relative units include:

  • %: Percentage – relative to the parent element.
  • em: Relative to the font-size of the parent element.
  • rem: Relative to the root element’s font size, usually the element
  • vw: Viewport width – 1vw is equal to 1% of the viewport width.
  • Viewport height – 1vh is equal to 1% of the viewport height
  • vmin and vmax: Minimum and maximum of viewport height and width, respectively.
